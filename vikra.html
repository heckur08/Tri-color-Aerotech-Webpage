At a Glance

A quadcopter, short for a quadrotor helicopter, is a versatile unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) with a four-rotor propulsion and control system. Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), commonly known as drones, have witnessed remarkable advancements in recent years, evolving beyond recreational and hobbyist use to play pivotal roles in various professional applications. One notable category within the drone landscape is the Heavy Lift Drones, designed and optimized for the capability to carry substantial payloads, making them well-suited for tasks that demand lifting heavy equipment or performing extended flights. This project delves into the design, construction, and application of a Heavy Lift Drone, specifically tailored for surveillance purposes in India. The need for such specialized drones arises from the growing demand for robust, versatile aerial platforms capable of lifting payloads up to 1kg while maintaining stable flight characteristics. Surveillance tasks, ranging from security monitoring to reconnaissance missions, can benefit significantly from the aerial perspective that heavy-lift drones provide. The project aims to explore the key components, design considerations, and technological aspects involved in the construction of a heavy lift drone, addressing challenges associated with stability, power distribution, and payload attachment. The surveillance aspect adds an extra layer of complexity, requiring integration of effective communication systems. By combining cutting-edge technology with a meticulous understanding of drone components and regulations, this project aspires to contribute to the field of aerial surveillance, providing a cost-effective and efficient solution for remote-controlled heavy lift drones in the Indian context. The utilization of such drones in surveillance applications can enhance situational awareness, aid in disaster response, and improve overall security measures. As we embark on this exploration, it is crucial to adhere to regulatory guidelines set by the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) in India. This project report aims to document the step-by-step process, challenges faced, and lessons learned in constructing a drone for surveillance, fostering a deeper understanding of the intricacies involved in the realm of advanced drone technology.

Literature survey

A Quad-copter, or multi-rotor, drone, or Quad-rotor, is a simple flying mechanical vehicle that has four arms, and in each arm there is a motor attached to a propeller. Multicopters with three, six or eight arms are also possible, but work on the same principal as a Quad-copter. Two of the rotors turn clockwise, while the other two turn counter clockwise. Quad-copters are aerodynamically unstable, and require a flight computer to convert your input commands into commands that change the RPMs of the propellers to produce the desired motion. In a Quad-copter the lift is generated by the propellers. A helicopter uses its main rotor to generate lift, but also have the ability to vary the pitch of the rotor blades to generate control forces. The advancement of electronic technology in flight control computers, coreless or brushless motors, smaller microprocessors, batteries, accelerometers, cameras, and even GPS systems made it possible to design and fly quad-copters.

Hovering takes place when the upward lift balances the downward force of gravity. To make the drone to higher, the lift force needs to be greater than the force of gravity. This is achieved by increasing RPM on all four propellers simultaneously, which produces more lift. To decrease the altitude, the RPM is decreased on all four propellers simultaneously. On your controller, this is accomplished by pushing the left stick up or down, which increases or decreases the rotor RPM, which makes your quad-copter go up or down. In order to move the drone left, right, forwards, and backwards, we change the angle of the lifting force so it has a vertical and horizontal component. The vertical component still serves to keep the quad-copter in the air, while the horizontal component allows produces control thrust.

To make quad-copter move forward or backwards, we adjust the pitch using the right stick on the controller. What happens is that the front propellers decrease RPMs, while the back propellers increase RPMs. Now the lift force has a horizontal component which results in moving the quad-copter forward. The opposite happens to make your quad-copter go backwards. The front propeller RPM is increased, while the back propeller RPM is decreased. To move your quad-copter move sideways, a similar change in RPM takes place, but this time it is on the left and right propellers. To make your drone move to left, you need to point the lift force slightly to the left. This is done by decreasing the RPM on the left rotors, and increasing the RPM on the right side. A similar change in the rotor RPMs takes place to move your quad-copter to the left.

Yaw is bit trickier to visualize. If the main blades are rotating anti-clockwise, like in most quadcopters, the frame will start rotating clockwise due to torque reaction. This is a consequence of Newton’s Third Law of dynamics. The tail rotor balances out the torque reaction on the quadcopter frame and prevents the frame from rotating. On a quad-copter, if all four propellers rotated the same way, then the body of your quad-copter would rotate the opposite way. But it obviously doesn’t. the propellers rotate clockwise, and the other two rotate anti-clockwise. So one set of propellers produces a torque in one direction, but the other two propellers are producing a torque in the opposite direction. These cancel out, so your quad-copter does not rotate. For a yaw maneuver, we do want the quad-copter to rotate. This is done by reducing the RPM on one set of rotors, while increasing the RPM on the other set of rotors. Now there is a net torque in one direction, so your quad-copter rotates. To produce the opposite rotation, the RPMs on the propellers is simply reversed. The direction of the yaw maneuver is controlled by the left stick on the controller

There are four main forces that act on a quad-copter:


  • Gravity - this should be obvious to everyone. This is the force that pulls the quadcopter down due to its mass.
  • Lift - this is the upward reaction force on the quad-copter due to the propellers.
  • Thrust – this is the horizontal reaction force on the quad-copter due to the propellers
  • Drag – this is the backward force on the quad-copter due to air. Imagine being in a car, and putting your hand out the window. The air hitting your hand is trying to push back.

    General Rules for Flying a Drone in India

    Based on our research and interpretation of the laws, here are the most important rules to know for flying a drone in India.
  • All drones except those in the Nano category must be registered and issued a Unique Identification Number (UIN).
  • A permit is required for commercial drone operations (except for those in the Nano category flown below 50 feet and those in the Micro category flown below 200 feet).
  • Drone pilots must maintain a direct visual line of sight at all times while flying.
  • Drones cannot be flown more than 400 feet vertically.
  • Drones cannot be flown in areas specified as "No Fly Zones", which include areas near airports, international borders, Vijay Chowk in Delhi, State Secretariat Complex in State Capitals, strategic locations, and military installations.
  • Permission to fly in controlled airspace can be obtained by filing a flight plan and obtaining a unique Air Defense Clearance (ADC)/Flight Information Center (FIC) number.

    • Drone Categories in India


    Registration is required for all but the Nano category.
    • • Nano: Less than or equal to 250 grams (.55 pounds)
    • • Micro: From 250 grams (.55 pounds) to 2kg (4.4 pounds)
    • • Small: From 2kg (4.4 pounds) to 25kg (55 pounds)
    • • Medium: From 25kg (55 pounds) to 150kg (330 pounds)
    • • Large: Greater than 150kg (33 pounds)

    Required Drone Equipment in India

    Also worth noting is that India has specific requirements regarding the types of features a drone must have to be flown in India (excluding those in the Nano category). These mandatory requirements include:
    • • GPS
    • • Return-to-home (RTH)
    • • Anti-collision light
    • • ID plate
    • • A flight controller with flight data logging capability
    • • RF ID and SIM/No Permission No Takeoff (NPNT)

    Methodology

    Quadcopter consists of four rotors which are fixed at the end of the frame structure. In this case, a pair of rotors along the arms of the frame rotates in a clockwise direction while the remaining pair of rotors rotate in an anticlockwise direction.

    Due to this, the resultant torque acting on the airframe structure is zero.



    Basic Components for building a quadcopter:


    1. Frame:
      • Choose a sturdy frame capable of supporting heavy payloads.
      • Made of fiber and has an integrated PCB for soldering ESCs and battery wires.
      • Carbon fiber frames are commonly used for their strength-to-weight ratio.
    2. Motors and Propellers:
      • Motors:
        • Select powerful brushless motors with high thrust capability.
        • With max power of 920W and 1400Kv (rpm/v).
        • Compatible with 3s-5s LiPo batteries.
      • Propellers:
        • Choose large propellers that match the motors for efficient lift.
        • Pusher and puller pair type.
    3. Electronic Speed Controllers (ESCs):
      • Use ESCs that are compatible with the selected motors. Make sure they can handle the current required for heavy lifting.
      • It follows a speed reference signal and varies the switching rate of field effect transistors.
    4. Flight Controller:
      • A reliable flight controller is crucial for stability.
    5. Battery and Power Distribution:
      • Choosing a high-capacity LiPo battery with a voltage compatible with your motors and ESCs.
      • Implementing a reliable power distribution system to supply power to all components.
    6. Payload Mechanism:
      • Implement a secure payload attachment mechanism.
      • Gimbal can be used to control its directions (optional).
    7. Remote Control System:
      • Choose a reliable remote control system with sufficient range.
      • Consider using a telemetry system for real-time data feedback.
      • The modulation scheme used between transmitter and receiver is pulse position modulation (PPM).

    ArduPilot is an open-source autopilot system that controls hardware like multi-copters, helicopters, aircraft, boats, submarines, and rovers. ArduPilot boards are designed to fit inside drones and connect to components like GPS, radio, and speed controllers. The ArduPilot flight control board includes a Ground Control Station (GCS) interface that can run on a PC or mobile device.
